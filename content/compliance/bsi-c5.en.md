The **BSI C5** (Cloud Computing Compliance Criteria Catalogue) is a German federal standard published by the BSI that defines minimum security requirements for cloud service providers. First released in 2016 and updated to **C5:2020** (the current operative version, with C5:2025 in development), it structures its criteria across **17 domains** aligned with ISO/IEC 27001 Annex A — covering organization, personnel, asset management, cryptography, operations security, communications security, supplier relationships, incident management, and compliance, among others. C5 is designed as an **attestation standard** (not a certification): providers undergo a **Type 2 audit** by an independent auditing firm (under IDW PS 880 or ISAE 3000), which verifies both the design and operational effectiveness of security controls over a period of at least six months. While historically voluntary, C5 is now **effectively mandatory** in two key domains: since 1 July 2025, cloud providers processing healthcare data must hold a valid C5 Type 2 attestation under §393 SGB V (Social Code, Fifth Book), and the revised BSI-KritisV (2024) requires KRITIS operators to use C5-attested cloud services in security-relevant contexts. Public-sector procurement in Germany also increasingly demands C5 attestation. The upcoming C5:2025 introduces sub-criteria aligned with EUCS and adds requirements for AI, container management, supply chain security, and post-quantum readiness, with mandatory application expected for assessments starting January 2027.

Red Hat enables cloud service providers and their customers to satisfy C5 requirements at the platform layer. For CSPs building on Red Hat technology, the C5 criteria around **cryptography** (domain 10) are addressed by RHEL's FIPS 140-3 validated modules and system-wide crypto policies; **operations security** (domain 12) maps to Red Hat's Compliance Operator for continuous configuration compliance, Ansible for automated patching and hardening, and RHACS for runtime threat detection; **supplier relationships** (domain 15) align with Red Hat's transparent supply chain — SBOMs, Sigstore artifact signing, and CSAF/VEX vulnerability feeds. C5's **corresponding criteria** (Korrespondierende Kriterien), which define the cloud customer's responsibilities at the interface with the provider, are also relevant: Red Hat's documentation of shared responsibility models for OpenShift Dedicated and ROSA helps customers understand exactly which C5 controls they inherit from the provider versus those they must implement themselves. For German healthcare organizations subject to the §393 SGB V mandate, running workloads on a C5-attested cloud infrastructure built on RHEL and OpenShift provides a clear compliance path, with Red Hat's layered security architecture contributing the evidence needed for the provider's Type 2 audit report.
