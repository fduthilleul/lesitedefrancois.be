**SecNumCloud** is a security qualification ("Visa de sécurité") issued by **ANSSI** (Agence Nationale de la Sécurité des Systèmes d'Information), France's national cybersecurity agency. Created in 2016 and currently in version **3.2** (published March 2022), it is the most demanding cloud security standard in France. SecNumCloud applies to cloud service providers offering **IaaS, PaaS, SaaS, or CaaS** (Container as a Service) and evaluates them against more than **360 requirements** spanning 14 domains structured on ISO/IEC 27001 Annex A, plus prescriptive additions covering hardening, multi-factor authentication, encryption, penetration testing, and data localization. The qualification is **voluntary** in principle — no law forces all cloud providers to obtain it — but it is **effectively mandatory** for providers serving French public administration, Opérateurs d'Importance Vitale (OIV), and entities handling sensitive government data, as French procurement policy (the "doctrine cloud de confiance") requires the use of SecNumCloud-qualified providers. Version 3.2's most significant addition is **Article 19.6**, which mandates that qualified providers be headquartered in the EU, owned by European entities (individual non-EU shareholding ≤24 %, collective ≤39 %), and be immune from non-European extraterritorial legislation such as the US CLOUD Act or FISA. SecNumCloud is the model upon which France advocates for the "high+sovereignty" tier in the EU-wide EUCS scheme. Qualification is valid for 3 years with annual audits conducted by PASSI-accredited assessors.

Red Hat is not itself a cloud service provider seeking SecNumCloud qualification, but it is a critical enabling layer for providers who are. The SecNumCloud ecosystem relies heavily on Red Hat technology: qualified providers like OVHcloud, Outscale (Dassault Systèmes), Cloud Temple, and the newly qualified S3NS (Thales partnership, SecNumCloud 3.2 obtained December 2025) all build their platforms on Red Hat software — RHEL as the host OS, OpenShift as the container orchestration platform, and Ansible for infrastructure automation. ANSSI's own published recommendations for deploying SecNumCloud-eligible IaaS explicitly reference OpenStack on RHEL as a reference architecture. Red Hat's technical capabilities map directly to SecNumCloud requirements: FIPS-mode cryptography and system-wide crypto policies satisfy encryption mandates; SELinux provides the mandatory access control and environment isolation the standard requires; the Compliance Operator and OpenSCAP enable automated, auditable conformity checks against ANSSI's hardening guides; and Red Hat's entirely open-source, European-operated support infrastructure (with no dependency on non-EU jurisdiction) helps providers demonstrate the sovereignty guarantees of Article 19.6. For organizations building SecNumCloud-qualified offerings, Red Hat provides the "composability" advantage introduced in version 3.2 — because the underlying RHEL/OpenShift platform is already used in qualified environments, a SaaS provider can focus its qualification efforts on its application layer rather than re-certifying the entire stack.
